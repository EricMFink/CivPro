## Scenario 

Several years ago, Marcus Neff, a resident of California, heard about a new federal law, the Donation Land Act ("DLA"), under which individuals could acquire one acre of federal property in Oregon. Neff contacted John Mitchell, a lawyer in Oregon, to prepare and submit a DLA application on his behalf. Mitchell agreed and told Neff his fee would be $300. After receiving notification that the application was approved, Mitchell sent Neff a bill for his legal services. To Neff's surprise, the bill was for $3000. When Neff called Mitchell demanding an explanation, Mitchell said that work turned out to be much more complicated and time consuming than he anticipated. Neff told Mitchell, "I don't believe that for one minute. You're just trying to cheat me. I'm not paying you one penny, and I'm going to tell everyone I know what a sleazebag you are." Hearing nothing further from Mitchell after that phone call, Neff went on with his life. 

A month ago, Neff finally travelled to Oregon to see his property. When he arrived, he was astonished to find that it was surrounded by a fence, with a sign reading "Private Property -- No Trespassing" posted at the gate. After asking around, he learned that someone named Sylvester Pennoyer claimed to own the property. Neff contacted Pennoyer, who explained that he'd bought the property from Mitchell a few years ago. According to Pennoyer, Mitchell acquired the property in a court-ordered sale, pursuant to a default judgment in a lawsuit that Mitchell filed against Neff for non-payment of legal fees. 

Neff suspects that Pennoyer and Mitchell had colluded to steal the property. He wants to bring a lawsuit to establish that the property belongs to him and to hold Pennoyer and Mitchell liable for their scam. 

## Claims 

1. Quiet Title: Neff v. Pennoyer 
2. Fraud: Neff v. Mitchell and Pennoyer. 
3. Nonpayment of Legal Fees: Mitchell v. Neff
