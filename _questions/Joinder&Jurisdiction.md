## Scenario 

Several years ago, Marcus Neff, a resident of California, heard about a new federal law, the Donation Land Act ("DLA"), under which individuals could acquire one acre of federal property in Oregon. Neff contacted John Mitchell, a lawyer in Oregon, to prepare and submit a DLA application on his behalf. Mitchell agreed and told Neff his fee would be $300. After receiving notification that the application was approved, Mitchell sent Neff a bill for his legal services. To Neff's surprise, the bill was for $3000. When Neff called Mitchell demanding an explanation, Mitchell said that work turned out to be much more complicated and time consuming than he anticipated. Neff told Mitchell, "I don't believe that for one minute. You're just trying to cheat me. I'm not paying you one penny, and I'm going to tell everyone I know what a sleazebag you are." Making good on his threat, Neff posts reviews on various online websites, saying "WARNING: If you're looking for a lawyer in Oregon, don't hire Mitchell. He's a crook, a liar, and a bigamist." 

A month ago, Neff finally travelled to Oregon to see his property. When he arrived, he was astonished to find that it was surrounded by a fence, with a sign reading "Private Property -- No Trespassing" posted at the gate. After asking around, he learned that someone named Sylvester Pennoyer, who lives in Idaho, claimed to own the property. Neff called Pennoyer, who said that he bought the property from Mitchell a few years ago from Mitchell, who had listed it for sale on website call Quillo.com. After communicating with Mitchell via the website's instant messaging service, Pennoyer agreed to buy the property for $1000, which he sent to Mitchell by electronic transfer using an app called Xenno. Pennoyer told Neff he'd asked  Mitchell why he was willing to sell the property for so little, when it was worth about $25,000. According to Pennoyer, Mitchell explained that he'd acquired the property for $100 at a court-ordered sale to satisfy a default judgment in a suit that Mitchell brought against a former client for non-payment of legal fees. 

Believing that he has been the victim of an elaborate swindle, Neff contacts a lawyer about bringing a lawsuit to establish that he is the rightful owner of the property and to seek compensation for being defrauded.
