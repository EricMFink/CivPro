1. Bob Roberts is running for President as the Monster Raving Loony Party candidate. He qualified for inclusion on the general election ballot in the State of Cackalacky by submitting petitions signed by the requisite number of eligible voters. Two months before the election, concluding that he has no chance to win, Roberts decides to terminate his campaign and endorses another candidate instead. He sends the Cackaclacky Secretary of State (the state official in charge of certifying candidates and printing ballots) a notice of withdrawal as a candidate. The Secretary of State rejects the notice, because it is filed two weeks after the deadline for withdrawal under state law. 

Roberts files a mandamus[^Hypo1] suit in Cackalacky state court, seeking an order directing the Secretary of State to accept his notice of withdrawal. After a hearing, the court enters an order dismissing the suit on the grounds that the Secretary of State properly rejected the notice as untimely. 

Roberts then files a suit in federal court, seeking an injunction prohibiting the Cackalacky Secretary of State from printing ballots with his name included as a candidate. The Secretary of State moves to dismiss on grounds of claim preclusion. How should the court rule? See Kennedy v. Benson (E.D. Mich. Sept. 18, 2024)

[^Hypo1]: 
  {-} "A (writ of) mandamus is an order from a court to an inferior government official ordering the government official to properly fulfill their official duties or correct an abuse of discretion." [Wex Legal Dictionary](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/mandamus)


2. Before a copyright holder may bring a civil action for infringement, they must register their copyright claim with the U.S. Copyright Office. 17 U.S. Code § 411. 
 
A sues B for copyright infringement. The court dismisses the suit on the grounds that A had not satisfied the registration requirement. After completing the registration process, A then brought a new suit for copyright infringement against B. Does claim preclusion bar the suit? See Foss v. Marvic (2024)

3. Plaintiff sues Defendant in NY state court, asserting claims under NY state law. The court dismisses on two grounds: (1) that Chinese Law, not NY law, governs and that Plaintiff's claims are not recognized under Chinese Law, and (2) even if NY law applies, the statute of limitations on Plaintiff's claims has run out. 

Plaintiff then files a new suit in federal court. Does claim preclusion bar the suit? See Beijing New Cloud v. IBM (2d Cir. 2024)
