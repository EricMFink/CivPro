Mrs. Claypool, an aspiring society matron, hears that the director of the New York Opera Company, Herman Gottlieb, is seeking a patron. Claypool believes this is a perfect way to enhance her standing in society. She hires Otis Driftwood to act as her agent in negotiating a deal with Gottlieb, under which Claypool will donate $500,000 to the Opera Company so it can hire Rodolfo Lassparri, the greatest tenor in the world. In exchange, Gottlieb agrees to name the Opera House after Claypool. 

Driftwood goes to meet Lassparri, intending to sign him to a contract with the Opera Company to close the deal between Claypool & Gottlieb. Along the way, he meets Fiorello, a genial con artist, who tricks Driftwood into signing Lassparri's rival, Baroni, instead. When Gottlieb discovers what has happened, he renegs on his agreement to name the Opera House after Claypool. 

A furious Claypool sues Gottlieb for breach of contract, seeking restitution of the $500,000 she donated to the Opera Company. She also sues Driftwood for breach of contract & fraud over his role in the failure of the Opera Company deal, seeking $50,000 (the amount she paid Driftwood for his services as her agent) plus punitive damages. Claypool brings her suit in federal court (assume that the court would have personal jurisdiction over all parties and subject matter jurisdiction over all claims).

<p class="case-h1">Part A</p>

1. Is joinder of Gottlieb and Driftwood as defendants proper under the FRCP?

_Answer:_ Joinder is proper under Rule 20(a)(2). Claypools claims against Gottlieb & Driftwood arise out of the same transaction: they are logically related as part of the Opera Company deal. Those claims will also share at at least some common question(s) of law or fact, e.g. about signing Lasparri to sing for the Opera. 

2. Assume that Claypool sues only Gottlieb. Gottlieb's defense is that his promise to name the Opera House after Claypool was contingent upon signing Lasparri to a contract with the Opera Company, and that Driftwood's failure to fulfill that part of the deal means that Gottlieb is not contractually obligated to honor his promise to Claypool. Is Driftwood a person required to be joined if feasible under the FRCP? 

_Answer:_ Driftwood is not a party who must be joined if feasible under Rule 19. Claypool's claim against Gottlieb seeks money damages for breach of the contract between those two parties. Facts about Driftwood's failure to fulfil his contract with Gottlieb might be relevant to Gootlieb's defense, and Dritwood might be called as a witness. But the court can fully adjudicate whether Gottlieb is liable to Claypool and award damages, without impairing Driftwood's interests and without leaving Gottlieb or Claypool unfairly subject to conflicting or multiple liabilities. 

3. Assume instead that Claypool sues only Dritwood. May Gottlieb intervene as a plaintiff to assert a claim against Driftwood for failure to sign Lasparri to a contract with the Opera Company? 

_Answer:_ Gottlieb could intervene under Rule 24(b)(1), since he has a claim that shares a common question of law or fact with Claypool's claim against Driftwood, i.e. regarding the failure to sign Lasparri. 

<p class="case-h1">Part B</p>

Assume that Claypool sued both Gottlieb & Driftwood and that their joinder as co-defendants was proper under the FRCP. 

For each of the following additional claims, explain whether joinder is proper under the FRCP: 

1. A claim by Driftwood against Claypool for breach of contract, seeking $25,000 he contends she still owes him for his services under their contract.

_Answer:_ This is a valid crossclaim under Rule 13(g), because it arises from the same transaction (the Opera Company deal) as Claypool's claims against the two co-defendants. 

2. A claim by Driftwood against Claypool for battery, alleging that she poured a bowl of hot soup over his head when he clumsily attempted to woo her during a party to celebrate the ill-fated Opera deal. 

_Answer:_ This is a permissive counterclaim under Rule 13(b). It doesn't arise from the same transaction as Claypool's claim against Driftwood, because it's not logically related to the Opera Company deal. 

3. A claim by Gottlieb against Driftwood, alleging that the Opera Company lost 50% of its anticipated season ticket sales because of the failure to sign Lassparri. 

_Answer:_ This is a valid crossclaim, because it also arises from the same transaction (the Opera Company deal). 

4. A claim by Gottlieb against Driftwood for breach of contract in an unrelated business transaction in which Driftwood was to supply the Opera Company with costumes. 

_Answer:_ On its own, this would not be a valid crossclaim, because it isn't logically related to the Opera Company deal. But, if Gottlieb also asserts the crossclaim in Question 3, Rule 18 would then allow him to join this claim even though it arises from a separate T/O.

5. A claim by Driftwood against Fiorello and Baroni, asserting that if Driftwood is found liable to Claypool or Gottlieb for his failure to sign Lasparri, Fiorello and Baroni must cover all or part of any damages Driftwood is ordered to pay Claypool or Gottlieb, because it was their fault Driftwood failed to sign Lasparri. 

_Answer:_ This is a valid third party claim under Rule 14, because Driftwood is seeking contribution or indemnification from Fiorello and Baroni on Claypool's underlying claim against Driftwood. 

6. A claim by Driftwood against Fiorello and Baroni, seeking $25,000 against them, jointly and severally, for defrauding him into signing Baroni to the Opera contract. The $25,000 represents the commission Driftwood expected to earn by signing Lasparri. 

_Answer:_ This would not be a valid third party claim under Rule 14, because it's not a claim for contribution or indemnification. But, if Driftwood joined Fiorello & Baroni as third party defendants for contribution or indemnification, Rule 18 would then also allow him to assert these claims against them. 

7. A claim by Baroni against Driftwood for failure to pay Baroni in accordance with the contract that Driftwood signed. 

_Answer:_ Joinder would be proper under Rule 14(a)(2). If Baroni's claim arises from the same T/O as Claypool's underlying claim (i.e. because the contract between Baroni and Driftwood was in furtherance of the Opera House deal), this would be a compulsory counterclaim under Rule 14(a)(2)(A)/13(a); alternatively, if Baroni's claim arises from a distinct T/O (i.e. because Driftwood's failure to pay Baroni isn't really logically related to the underlying dispute between Claypool and Driftwood), it would be a permissive counterclaim under Rule 14(a)(2)(B)/13(b).
